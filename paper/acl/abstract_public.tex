How do language models represent a relational operation ---
\emph{currency-of}, \emph{capital-of} --- as distinct from the entity it
applies to? We separate three levels of evidence.
\textbf{Representation:} a two-way effects decomposition of the
query-position state is \textasciitilde90\% additive in operand and
operator across Qwen3-1.7B/8B and Gemma-2-9B, and the steering vector is
\emph{identically} the decomposition\textquotesingle s operator main
effect. \textbf{Causal influence:} adding the operator difference flips
the target-vs-source answer margin for every ordered relation pair in
all three models; the decisive nulls --- directions rebuilt under
\textbf{permuted relation labels}, and random directions \textbf{inside
the operator subspace} --- abolish the effect. \textbf{Behavioral
sufficiency:} a per-layer residual trajectory \emph{composed} from the
decomposition\textquotesingle s parts,
\texttt{\ensuremath{\mu}\ +\ operand\ +\ operator}, patched at the query position,
makes the model \emph{produce} the target answer at its own accuracy
level; components built without the target cell still generate, and
swapping the operand component redirects the answer. The interaction
term adds nothing detectable --- and the earlier failure of additive
steering to generate was a \textbf{dose artifact}: an
out-of-sample-calibrated dose generates at the same level, while
overdosing inflates the margin as it pushes the state off-manifold. The
structure generalizes (held-out operands, re-worded prompts, a second
domain), separates the operation from its surface word, and does not
emerge for arithmetic or logic under the identical pipeline.
