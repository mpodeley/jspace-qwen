\section{Introduction}

A line of work shows that a \emph{task} or \emph{relation} can be
captured by a single addable vector in an LLM\textquotesingle s residual
stream (task vectors, \citealp{hendel2023icl}; function vectors, \citealp{todd2024function}) and that many relations are approximately linear operators (LRE,
\citealp{hernandez2024lre}; \citealp{merullo2024vector}). What has not been
characterized is the \textbf{joint structure of operator and operand}:
whether the operation \emph{factorizes} from its argument, whether that
factorization \emph{generalizes}, whether the operation is separable
from the \emph{word it produces}, and whether any of this holds
\emph{beyond} relational facts, in arithmetic and logic. We answer these
on Qwen3 and Gemma-2. A closing discussion (\S{}5) offers a morphological
reading --- relational computation as \textbf{declension} --- as an
organizing metaphor for the asymmetries we observe.

\section{Related work and what is new
here}

\textbf{Extracting relational concepts.} The closest prior work is Wang
et al. (2024), who locate hidden states that express a relation
separately from its subject, transplant these relational representations
between subjects, and rewrite relations across 22 relation types and
several models. We instead characterize the \textbf{joint geometry} of
relation and entity: we quantify its additive and interaction components
(the two-way ANOVA with a measured fusion term), run exhaustive
all-pairs swaps against matched-norm nulls, and test whether relation
directions generalize causally across held-out entities, prompt
contexts, architectures, and task domains --- including the domains
where the factorization fails.

\textbf{Operation as an addable vector.} Task Vectors (\citealp{hendel2023icl}) and Function Vectors (\citealp{todd2024function}) extract one
vector per ICL task and add/patch it to trigger the task. In-Context
Vectors (\citealp{liu2024icv}) and ActAdd (\citealp{turner2023actadd}) steer a
task/style similarly. These bundle operator \emph{and} operand into one
unfactored "task" direction and (for FVs) show \emph{partial} cross-task
arithmetic without null controls. We contribute the
\textbf{factorization} (operator \ensuremath{\oplus} operand), an \textbf{exhaustive
all-pairs swap with matched-norm nulls}, and a \textbf{held-out-operand
generalization} test.

\textbf{Relation as a linear operator.} LRE (\citealp{hernandez2024lre}) fits a full affine map \texttt{W\textperiodcentered{}s\ +\ b} per relation. That
represents the relation as a \emph{monolithic} operator; we show an
\textbf{additive operator \emph{direction}} that is arithmetically
composable and \textbf{factorable from the operand}, and we quantify the
operand/operator/interaction variance split, which LRE does not.

\textbf{Additive factual recall.} Summing Up the Facts (\citealp{chughtai2024summing}) decomposes factual recall into additive \emph{circuits} (subject
vs relation contributions). Ours is a \textbf{representational two-way
ANOVA of the residual state} with an explicit, measured
\textbf{interaction/fusion term} --- a different object.

\textbf{Attribute subspaces / reading position.} RAVEL (\citealp{huang2024ravel}) disentangles multiple \emph{attributes of one entity} into
subspaces (operators sharing an operand); we measure the orthogonal
axis, \textbf{operator vs operand}. The operand\ensuremath{\to}operator shift along the
sequence restates the subject-enrichment \ensuremath{\to} attribute-extraction dynamics
of \citet{geva2023dissecting} in operator/operand terms --- our least
novel point, included as confirmation.

\textbf{Global workspace and the Jacobian lens.} \citet{gurnee2026workspace} introduce the Jacobian lens and identify a
mid-depth "global workspace" band of the residual stream. We adopt their
depth band as the site of intervention and their lens as scaffolding;
our contribution is orthogonal --- the operator/operand structure of
what that band \emph{holds} --- and we additionally report a controlled
null on the lens\textquotesingle s readout advantage for our task
(\S{}4.8).

\textbf{Arithmetic and logic.} Arithmetic in LLMs is reported as a "bag
of heuristics" (\citealp{nikankin2025heuristics}) and Fourier/helical procedures
(\citealp{nanda2023grokking}; \citealp{zhong2023clock}; \citealp{kantamneni2025trig}), with
the operand \emph{value} cleanly linear (\citealp{gurnee2024space}). Truth
is a steerable direction (\citealp{marks2023geometry}) and logical-operator
heads exist (\citealp{hong2024implies}). No prior work reports a
\textbf{cross-domain operator/operand factorization}; we provide one and
show it \textbf{breaks down} for arithmetic and logic --- itself a
result.

\textbf{What is new here is the combination, not any single ingredient.}
That relations admit addable vectors (task/function vectors) and linear
decoders (LRE) is established; what has not been done is the joint,
quantified analysis: (1) an operator \ensuremath{\oplus} operand \textbf{factorization of
the residual state itself} (two-way ANOVA with a measured
interaction/fusion term and operator/operand subspace angles); (2) an
\textbf{exhaustive all-pairs causal swap} with matched-norm nulls; (3)
\textbf{held-out-operand generalization} as the test separating a
transferable direction from interpolation among build examples; (4) the
\textbf{operation-vs-realization dissociation} (language \ensuremath{\neq} demonym as
directions despite identical output); and (5) a \textbf{cross-domain
test} showing the factorization is specific to relational retrieval and
breaks down for arithmetic and logic. The declension/case reading is our
\emph{presentation} of these measurements --- an organizing metaphor
that anticipates the observed asymmetries (syncretism at the exponent,
fusion in the interaction term) --- not an additional empirical claim.

\section{Method}

\textbf{Setup.} Qwen3-1.7B/8B throughout, plus Gemma-2-9B for the
cross-architecture replication (\S{}4.5); all run in bf16 on a single AMD
Strix Halo APU (environment details in the supplementary repository). A
relation is rendered into a template; the canonical frame is
\texttt{"The\ \{op\}\ of\ \{a\}\ is"}, and \S{}4.4 additionally uses two
paraphrase frames (question--answer and discourse-prefixed) that hold
the \texttt{\{op\}\ of\ \{a\}} unit fixed while varying the surrounding
frame, all ending in \emph{is} so answer scoring is comparable. Reading
position is the query token unless stated; the depth "workspace" band
(38--92\% of layers) is where \citet{gurnee2026workspace} locates the global
workspace and where we build operator directions.

\textbf{Operator direction.} For operator \texttt{k},
\texttt{v(k)\ =\ mean\_operand{[}\ h(operand,\ k)\ -\ mean\_op\ h(operand,\ \textperiodcentered{})\ {]}}
at the workspace layers --- the deviation of an operator from the
operand\textquotesingle s average over operators, averaged over operands
(the function-vector construction; implementation details in Appendix
A).

\textbf{Swap and controls.} Efficacy of \texttt{k\_A\ \ensuremath{\to}\ k\_B} is the
change in \texttt{logit(answer\_B)\ -\ logit(answer\_A)} at the query
position when \texttt{\ensuremath{\alpha}\textperiodcentered{}(v(k\_B)\ -\ v(k\_A))} is added over the band,
versus a \textbf{matched-norm random} direction. Answers are scored by
their distinguishing token (bare digit for arithmetic, since Qwen3
splits " 3" into {[}space, 3{]}); a tokenization guard drops, within
each operator pair, the operands whose two answers collide at that token
(for relations this leaves 4 of 12 operands on the syncretic
demonym\ensuremath{\leftrightarrow}language pairs and all 12 elsewhere).

\textbf{Factorization.}
\texttt{H{[}operand,\ operator{]}\ =\ \ensuremath{\mu}\ +\ operand(o)\ +\ operator(k)\ +\ interaction};
we report the variance share of each term and the principal angles
between the operand- and operator-subspaces (Appendix A).

\textbf{Generalization.} Build \texttt{v(op)} from half the operands;
test the swap on the held-out half. This distinguishes a genuine
operator from interpolation among the examples used to build it.

\textbf{Statistical treatment.} The 20 ordered swaps are \textbf{not} 20
independent observations: they are built from 5 operator directions
(each participating in 8 ordered pairs) and evaluated on the same shared
operand set (12 operands, 4 on the syncretic pairs after the
tokenization guard). We therefore report cluster-bootstrap percentile
intervals (10,000 replicates) at two levels. Within a pair, the
\textbf{operand} is the resampling unit (per-pair 95\% CIs). Across the
paradigm, the \textbf{operator} is the top-level cluster: a dyadic node
bootstrap resamples the operator set with replacement, weights each
ordered pair by the product of its endpoints\textquotesingle{}
multiplicities, and resamples operands within surviving pairs
(per-operand values are released as long-form artifacts; Appendix A).

\textbf{J-space controls.} We repeat readout-geometry in the J-lens
readout \texttt{unembed(J\textperiodcentered{}h)} vs the logit-lens readout
\texttt{unembed(h)}, and re-run efficacy with \textbf{spectrum-matched
random-projection} and \textbf{permuted-vocabulary} null lenses
(Appendix B).

\section{Results}

\subsection{Relational operators are manipulable
directions}

All 20 ordered operator swaps flip the \textbf{target-vs-source logit
margin} (the sign of \texttt{logit(answer\_B)\ -\ logit(answer\_A)} at
the distinguishing token; mean swap \textbf{+21} logit units on 1.7B,
\textbf{+25} on 8B) while the matched-norm random control is
\textasciitilde0. Representative (1.7B):

\centering\small

\toprule
swap & clean & +operator & +random \\

capital \ensuremath{\to} currency & -12.0 & \textbf{+33.0} & -2.5 \\
capital \ensuremath{\to} language & -8.5 & \textbf{+32.5} & +3.0 \\
continent \ensuremath{\to} language & -1.9 & \textbf{+23.0} & +1.4 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
}

Under the operator-level cluster bootstrap (\S{}3, the level that respects
that pairs share directions), the swap-random contrast is \textbf{+22.6,

both scales --- every replicate flips every pair. Per-pair
operand-bootstrap CIs never cross zero:

\caption{Per-pair distributions: swap values per operand with operand-bootstrap 95\% CIs, matched-norm random control, and clean baselines. No CI crosses zero; the weakest pairs are exactly the syncretic ones (demonym\ensuremath{\leftrightarrow}language and their neighbours).}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{Dose--response and collateral cost.} Sweeping the intervention
strength \ensuremath{\alpha} (1.7B) separates the effect into its parts: the
\textbf{operator-specific} component (swap - random) rises and
\textbf{saturates at \ensuremath{\approx}+23 by the default \ensuremath{\alpha} = 4}, while the matched-norm
random control contributes a \textbf{nonspecific} margin shift of \ensuremath{\approx}+6
that is flat in \ensuremath{\alpha} --- any large perturbation degrades the clean
answer\textquotesingle s dominance somewhat, which is why all headline
numbers are swap - random contrasts. The intervention is
\emph{answer}-surgical, not \emph{distribution}-surgical: with the same
hook active on unrelated WikiText, per-token KL(clean \ensuremath{\,\Vert\,} intervened)
grows from 7.9 nats (\ensuremath{\alpha} = 0.5) to 21 nats (\ensuremath{\alpha} = 12) --- 18.4 at the
default --- so the band-wide edit substantially distorts off-task text.
We report this as an honest cost of the band-wide, all-position
intervention rather than tuning it away; targeted (single-position)
variants are the natural mitigation and are left to future work.

\textbf{Minimal intervention, and margins vs. generation.} The band-wide
injection is not the minimal effective intervention: restricting the
same directions to a \textbf{single mid-workspace layer} still flips
\textbf{20/20 margins} (contrast +6.8 {[}+3.0, +8.9{]}) at 4.5\ensuremath{\times} lower
off-task cost (KL 4.6 vs. 20.6 nats/token); the middle half-band gives
+12.8 {[}+7.9, +16.3{]} at 10.9 nats. Greedy decoding sharpens the
picture: the additive injection --- at any width --- flips the
\emph{relative} margin but does not make the model emit the target
answer within three greedy tokens (0\% exact match; consistent with its
off-task distortion), whereas \textbf{replacing the query-position
residual with a real donor activation} (same operand, target relation
--- classic activation patching: one position, no averaged direction)
makes the model \emph{say} the target answer at \textbf{51\%},
essentially its own clean-prompt accuracy of 53\%. The averaged
difference direction manipulates preference; the state-level patch
reroutes the generated answer at the model\textquotesingle s competence
ceiling.

\subsection{Operation \ensuremath{\neq} realization
(syncretism)}

\emph{language-of} and \emph{demonym-of} emit the \textbf{same word}
(Italian) yet are \textbf{distinct operator directions}; the swap
between them is weak precisely because they share an exponent. A
\textbf{pure desinence} built from the shared-output pairs
(\texttt{mean{[}h(language)\ -\ h(demonym){]}}, exponent cancelled)
still installs the relation causally (1.7B: -3.6 \ensuremath{\to} +12.5). The
syncretism appears at the \emph{exponent} level, not the \emph{case}
level --- as in Latin declension.

\subsection{Operators generalize to held-out operands and across
prompt
frames}

\textbf{Held-out operands.} \texttt{v(op)} built on 6 operands and
applied to the 6 held-out operands still flips \textbf{20/20} swaps ---
a genuine, transferable operator, not interpolation. Operator-level
cluster bootstrap on the held-out contrast: \textbf{+20.0 {[}+10.8,
+29.5{]}} at 1.7B, \textbf{+22.8 {[}+14.0, +31.0{]}} at 8B; flip
fraction \textbf{1.00 {[}1.00, 1.00{]}} at both.

\textbf{Cross-frame transfer.} To rule out template echo we render every
relation in three paraphrase frames --- declarative
(\texttt{The\ \{op\}\ of\ \{a\}\ is}), question--answer
(\texttt{Q:\ What\ is\ the\ \{op\}\ of\ \{a\}?\ A:\ It\ is}), and
discourse-prefixed
(\texttt{It\ is\ well\ known\ that\ the\ \{op\}\ of\ \{a\}\ is}) --- and
test every build\ensuremath{\to}test frame combination: \texttt{v(op)} built on frame
\emph{i}, all-pairs swap run on frame \emph{j}. On 1.7B, \textbf{all 9
combinations flip 20/20} (180/180; off-diagonal transfer 120/120, mean
contrast \textbf{+24.2}), and the contrast is \emph{frame-invariant to
two decimals}: directions built on the declarative frame produce +22.6
on all three test frames. The clean baselines do shift across frames
(-7.0 / -8.1 / -7.9), confirming the frames are genuinely different
prompts; the operator\textquotesingle s causal effect does not. At 8B
the pattern replicates --- \textbf{100/100 flips over the tested
combinations (80/80 cross-frame, mean contrast +28.7)}, with the
declarative-built direction again frame-invariant (+26.0 / +26.0 /
+26.0). The operator direction is invariant to the prompt
\textbf{context} around the fixed \texttt{\{op\}\ of\ \{a\}} unit.

\textbf{Cross-lexicalization transfer.} The stronger question is whether
the direction survives changing the \emph{wording of the relation
itself}. We re-lexicalize every relation --- \emph{currency of \{a\}} \ensuremath{\to}
\emph{money used in \{a\}}, \emph{capital of \{a\}} \ensuremath{\to} \emph{seat of
government in \{a\}}, \emph{language of \{a\}} \ensuremath{\to} \emph{language
primarily spoken in \{a\}}, \emph{demonym} \ensuremath{\to} \emph{name for someone from
\{a\}}, \emph{continent of \{a\}} \ensuremath{\to} \emph{world region containing \{a\}}
--- and test both transfer directions. Directions built on the
of-phrasings flip the re-lexicalized prompts, and vice versa:
\textbf{40/40 at 1.7B (mean contrast +21.7) and 40/40 at 8B (+26.8)},
with transfer contrasts indistinguishable from within-formulation ones
(1.7B: +22.59 transferred vs. +22.62 within; operator-level 95\% CIs
overlap almost exactly). The clean baselines differ across formulations
--- the prompts are genuinely different --- but the
operator\textquotesingle s causal effect does not. The direction is a
property of the \textbf{relation}, not of its wording.

The gradient replicates and sharpens at \textbf{8B}: relational held-out

generalization test is what separates} a transferable operator direction
from memorized interpolation.

\subsection{Reconciling with a contrary report: add-N vs.
two-operand
arithmetic}

\citet{christ2025structure} report the \emph{opposite} sign for arithmetic: an
operator built from an \textbf{add-N} relation (a fixed addend
\texttt{N} applied to a single number) \textbf{does} generalize to
held-out relations. The two results do not conflict --- they cut
arithmetic along different axes. In add-N the operator \textbf{is the
addend \texttt{N}} over one numeric operand, so "generalizing across
\texttt{N}" is interpolation along the number line (the operand
\emph{value} is itself linear; \citealp{gurnee2024space}), not transfer of
a \emph{function}. Our \texttt{+\ \ensuremath{\times}\ -} cut varies the \textbf{function}
over two operands. Running both cuts on Qwen3
(\texttt{op\_core.py\ -\/-domain\ arith\_addN} vs \texttt{arithmetic};
\texttt{scripts/operator\_collinearity.py}) places add-N exactly between
relations and the genuine-function cut:

{
\begin{table*}[t]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{llr}
\toprule
cut & held-out generalization (1.7B / 8B) & operator-set collinearity
(top-1 var) \\
\midrule
relations (5 operations) & 20/20 \textperiodcentered{} 20/20 & 0.39 (spread paradigm) \\
\textbf{add-N} (operator = addend) & 5/12 \textperiodcentered{} 6/12 (+0.10 / +0.05) &
\textbf{0.76 (most 1-D / number-line)} \\
\texttt{+\ \ensuremath{\times}\ -} (operator = function) & 2/6 \textperiodcentered{} 1/6 (-0.43 / -0.55) &
0.64 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table*}
}

add-N generalizes \textbf{better} than \texttt{+\ \ensuremath{\times}\ -} (positive vs.
clearly negative) and its operator directions are the \textbf{most
collinear} of the three families --- 76\% of the operator-set variance
on a single line --- consistent with add-N being a linear numeric family
rather than a set of distinct operations. This reproduces Christ et
al.\textquotesingle s positive (their cut varies a linear parameter)
alongside our negative (ours varies the function) with no contradiction.
The caveat is quantitative: Qwen3 BPE restricts us to single-digit
results, so the add-N grid is small (5 operands) and its generalization
is weak in absolute terms --- the \textbf{ordering} (relations \ensuremath{\gg} add-N
\textgreater{} \texttt{+\ \ensuremath{\times}\ -}), not the magnitude, is the claim.

\subsection{This is causal structure, not a J-space readout
advantage}

This project began as a replication of the J-space readout claim of
\citealp{gurnee2026workspace}, whose "workspace" band we
intervene on throughout. Under matched controls --- including a
spectrum-matched random projection --- the Jacobian-lens readout showed
\textbf{no advantage} over the logit lens on any of four comparisons
(Appendix B). The structure reported here is \textbf{causal}
organization of the residual stream, not a privileged readable subspace.

\section{Discussion: relational computation as
declension}

The measurements invite a morphological reading, offered as an
organizing metaphor rather than an additional claim. In a declining
language a noun\textquotesingle s role is marked by a case ending on a
stem --- and each structural feature of a case system has a measured
counterpart here. The same stem takes different endings (one country,
five operator directions); the same ending applies across stems
(held-out-operand transfer, \S{}4.4); two cases can share a surface form
without being the same case (\textbf{syncretism}: \emph{language-of} and
\emph{demonym-of} both emit "Italian", yet are distinct directions and
their mutual swap is the weakest of the paradigm, \S{}4.3); and stem and
ending do not compose perfectly (\textbf{fusion}: the
\textasciitilde10\% interaction term, \S{}4.2). The exponent-free
\textbf{desinence} is the metaphor\textquotesingle s sharpest prediction
--- a case marker stripped of its surface form should still be causally
functional, and it is (\S{}4.3). The metaphor also marks its own limits:
arithmetic and logic operators do not decline (\S{}4.6); whatever those
domains use, it is not a case system.

\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Scale and family.} 1.7--9B models from two families (Qwen3,
  Gemma 2) --- both decoder-only transformers; no \ensuremath{\geq}30B model. Relational
  linearity holds for a subset of relations even in-domain (cf. LRE
  \textasciitilde48\%).
\item
  \textbf{Relation coverage.} Five hand-chosen country relations,
  English-only. Prompt variation covers three context frames and a full
  re-lexicalization of every relation (\S{}4.4); more relations, other
  entity types, and other languages remain open.
\item
  \textbf{Arithmetic coverage.} Qwen3 BPE splits multi-digit numbers, so
  we restrict to single-digit results; the arithmetic null is on that
  regime and at 1.7B/8B, where arithmetic competence is itself limited.
\item
  \textbf{Reading-position result} restates known
  subject-enrichment\ensuremath{\to}attribute-extraction dynamics.
\item
  \textbf{No J-space claim.} The readout-geometry difference is
  1.7B-specific; the paper stands on the causal factorization, not on a
  J-lens readout advantage.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

An LLM represents a relational operation as a causally manipulable
operator direction that factorizes from the operand, generalizes to
unseen operands, and separates the operation from the word that realizes
it --- a structure well described as \textbf{declension}. This
factorization is not universal: it is clean for relational retrieval and
breaks down for arithmetic and logic, whose operators are entangled with
their operands. The structure is causal; it is not a more-readable
subspace.
